Quick Introduction / Abstract (Kotaro)
2. Context (Basically a half-pager summary of context, conditions of success and evaluation in proposal) (Kotaro)
Urban traffic congestion costs Toronto an estimated $6 billion annually in lost productivity [1]. This project is a two-model traffic optimization system for a corridor of 12 to 15 intersections in Toronto, specifically aiming to reduce total delay and help predict traffic change due to a change in the infrastructure. There are two baseline signal cycling systems which the model aims to beat; Fixed-Time and Max-Pressure. Fixed-Time is simply letting intersections run blindly on a predefined timer. This is the most basic (and cheapest) system that should be easy to beat with any kind of adaptive signalling. Max-Pressure is a decentralized (i.e. each intersection has no access to global traffic data) heuristic based algorithm that prioritizes relieving the road with higher traffic. While this model performs well under regular traffic conditions it was trained on, it fails to adapt under irregular conditions (e.g. lane closure, sports event, subway disruptions).  
The first layer, ASCE (Adaptive Signal Control Engine), is a decentralized model, where each intersection acts as an independent agent. It takes the current state at each signal intersection, including but not limited to queue lengths, incoming arrivals, approach speeds, and time of day. It then chooses a safe next signal decision stating the next phase to be served and duration of the green signal. This model aims to reduce delay and prevent queues from backing into other streets, even under irregular conditions. 
The second layer, PIRA (Planning Infrastructure Response Analyzer), models the effect of construction, lane closures, and new public transit lines on traffic. For each scenario, the model would estimate how demand and road capacity will change and then recommend practical updates to signal timing and phasing, using insight from the first layer. This model aims to solve the problem of turning proposed projects into data-driven signal changes that keep traffic moving smoothly during and after the disruption.
3. Data and Simulation Plan ((see data-setup branch)[https://github.com/yashnkapadia/ece324-TANGO/tree/data-setup] for data pipeline directory and scripts)
This project uses two layers of data. The first layer is the City of Toronto Traffic Volumes Multimodal Intersection Turning Movement Counts (TMC) dataset, which records motor vehicle, bicycle, and pedestrian volumes at intersections in 15-minute intervals. The raw TMC dataset contains approximately 800,000 samples dating back to 2000. Each record includes the intersection location, date, time period, approach direction, turning movement, and vehicle count. The second layer is a large simulated training dataset built on top of a real OpenStreetMap road network using the SUMO microsimulation platform, calibrated against the TMC volumes to produce millions of reinforcement learning training samples.
3.1 Study Area
This project focuses on signalized intersections along Dundas Street West in Toronto, Ontario, Canada, from the intersection at University Avenue to the intersection at Bathurst Street. The signalized intersections selected on this corridor are shown in the picture below:
[figure: sumo_scenario_signalized_intersections.png (caption: Generated SUMO scenario showing project area focus (the selected area is inspected using sumolib and annotated using matplotlib to highlight signalized intersections). The red markers indicate the locations of signalized intersections along the corridor].
This corridor was chosen because TMC data are available for it, it includes a streetcar route that is relevant for transit scenarios in the next phase of the project, and it is small enough (5 to 20 intersections) to iterate quickly while being large enough to capture signal coordination effects.
To support the simulated training dataset, the study area is first converted into a SUMO road network and then checked and corrected for signal control. The road network is created with the SUMO OSM Web Wizard, which pulls a bounding box from OpenStreetMap and produces a SUMO network file (osm.net.xml) that includes road segments (edges), intersections (junctions), lanes, connections, and traffic light programs. The bounding box covers roughly 200 meters north and south of Dundas Street between Bathurst and University. After export, a mapping script links each known intersection to its corresponding SUMO junction by projecting geographic coordinates with sumolib and matching to the nearest junction ID. The result is an intersection map CSV that connects real intersection names to SUMO junction identifiers.
Because traffic light programs imported from OpenStreetMap are often incomplete or unsafe, the network is also audited and updated before running experiments. A TLS generator script scans every traffic light in the network, labels incoming connections as east-west or north-south based on edge angle, and writes a corrected additional file (tls_overrides.add.xml) with two-phase signal plans. Each plan applies a minimum green of 7 seconds, followed by 3 seconds of yellow and 2 seconds of all-red clearance. Dundas Street is treated as the main arterial and is assigned 30 seconds of green, while cross streets receive 20 seconds.
3.2 Demand Generation and Calibration for Input to MAPPO
For ASCE, the MAPPO algorithm takes as input the network (network.net.xml) and vehicle demand (demand.rou.xml). For this interim stage of the project, demand is generated only for vehicles. The network file is generated when the study area is selected in the OSM Web Wizard (described in Section 3.1). The demand file is generated in SUMO using TMC data from the City of Toronto Open Data portal (filtered to the intersections of interest on Dundas Street West). Turning volumes per approach and per movement (left, through, right) are extracted and converted into SUMO flow elements that specify vehicle injection rates between entry and exit edges of the network for an AM peak-hour period.
An iterative calibration loop then runs for one hour to collect simulated approach volumes via SUMO’s edge data output and to calculate the GEH statistic for validating each approach using:
[
\mathrm{GEH} = \sqrt{\frac{2(M - C)^2}{M + C}},
]
where (M) is the simulated count and (C) is the TMC count.
Entry flows are scaled with 50% damping until GEH is below 5 for at least 85% of approaches, with a maximum of 10 iterations. 
3.3 Baseline Simulation and Logging for GNN Dataset 
The following subsection describes planned work for the next phase of the project.
Two baseline controllers will be run over the calibrated network (fixed-time and max-pressure). The fixed-time controller will follow the predefined phase plans without modification. The max-pressure controller will, at each decision point, select the phase whose green approaches have the largest total queue of halting vehicles. Both controllers will use the same logging infrastructure. Every 5 simulation seconds, for every signalized intersection, the pipeline will log one row containing:
intersection ID, simulation time step, halting vehicle counts on north-south and east-west approaches, vehicle counts per approach, mean speeds per approach, current phase index, time of day, the action taken (next phase and green duration), and outcome metrics including total delay, total queue length, and throughput.
Each row will form an ML sample of the form (state at time (t), action at time (t), state at time (t+1), metrics from (t) to (t+1)). For a one-hour run with 12 intersections, this will produce approximately 8,640 logged rows per controller per scenario. All logs will be saved as Apache Parquet files. All Parquet log files from baseline and scenario runs will be merged into a single ML-ready dataset keyed by (intersection ID, time step, scenario ID). Scenario metadata will be joined to provide context. The final consolidated dataset at data/final/dataset.parquet will contain all columns from the logging schema and will be ready for consumption by the GNN training modules. No ML models will be trained in this pipeline stage. The pipeline will be fully scripted across eight numbered Python scripts plus utility modules, with a defined commit plan of 12 incremental commits to ensure reproducibility at every stage. 4. Current Work on the Modelling (Aryan)

4.1 MAPPO Architecture
We adopt Multi-Agent Proximal Policy Optimization (MAPPO) [cite: mappo, Yu et al. 2022] with parameter sharing, following the CityLight architecture [cite: citylight]. Each of the 12 signalized intersections acts as an independent agent sharing a common policy network: a 2-layer ReLU MLP with 128 hidden units mapping local observations (queue lengths, vehicle counts, mean speeds, current phase, and time-of-day from sumo-rl) to a softmax distribution over legal signal phases. A centralized critic receives the concatenated observations from all agents to estimate state value during training only. Per-feature observation normalization is maintained via Welford running statistics. Full hyperparameters are listed in Appendix B.

4.2 Reward Design
Our initial approach used a time-loss reward r = -log(1 + delay), directly targeting the proposal's primary metric. However, this sparse, single-objective signal proved insufficient for convergence: MAPPO trained under this reward achieved 8,434 +/- 589 s person-time-loss -- worse than even the Fixed-Time baseline (8,040 +/- 564 s). The policy failed to discover useful signal coordination from delay feedback alone.

We replaced this with a multi-objective reward that combines delay reduction, throughput encouragement, and a throughput-fairness term across intersections (full equation in Appendix C). Under this reward, MAPPO improved to 7,101 +/- 248 s -- a 16% reduction over the time-loss reward and a clear improvement over Fixed-Time, though still 25% behind Max-Pressure. The richer reward landscape provides gradient signal even when delay changes are small, enabling the policy to learn meaningful coordination.

4.3 Experimental Setup
The study area is a Toronto OSM corridor with 12 signalized intersections and 70 TMC-calibrated car flows active over a 0-3600 s window (see Section 3 for data details). Episodes run for up to 300 s at 5 s decision intervals; demand exhausts at approximately 285 s. We evaluate three controllers -- MAPPO, Fixed-Time, and Max-Pressure -- each over 10 random seeds. MAPPO is trained for 30 episodes with observation normalization on an NVIDIA RTX 4070 Laptop GPU; total compute for all reported experiments is under 1 GPU-hour.

Max-Pressure dominates all metrics under nominal conditions. This is expected: it is provably throughput-optimal under stationary, single-commodity demand [cite: max_pressure_optimal, Varaiya 2013], which is exactly this benchmark. The Toronto corridor with 70 uniform car flows is the regime where Max-Pressure's local queue-balancing heuristic is near-optimal. No controller reaches the proposal's Jain fairness target of 0.8, indicating that delay inequality across vehicles is inherent to this network topology and demand pattern, not controller-specific.

The 25% gap motivates concrete next steps: (1) warm-start MAPPO from Max-Pressure imitation so it begins at least as good as the heuristic, (2) curriculum training with progressively harder scenarios (demand spikes, incidents, multi-modal flows) where Max-Pressure's single-commodity assumption breaks, and (3) extend training beyond 30 episodes -- the training curve (Panel A) shows the policy improving but not yet converged. The multi-objective reward already demonstrates that richer signals substantially improve learning; the next leverage point is the training environment, not the reward.

5. Future Actions (Kotaro)
As of this interim report, the following two actions are still required to achieve what the project proposes to achieve. 

Firstly, although ASCE has been tested to work, PIRA has not been implemented yet. This is the most significant part that requires future work. Additionally, although ASCE was trained on real traffic data (TMC) from the City of Toronto, only the data of personal automotives were included, not buses, pedestrians or any paralleling transit lines. For PIRA to work, those additional pieces of information will be crucial, and needs to be included in the training process.

Secondly, though ASCE (total delay of 5405.39 seconds) outperforms Fixed-Time (6184.94 s),  it still performs worse than max-pressure (4353.33s) under regular traffic as of now. Since ASCE is meant to be a more general model that can handle irregular traffic conditions and performs at least as well as Max-Pressure under regular conditions, the following can be done: initialize ASCE to behave the same as max-pressure so that it performs at least as well as max-pressure, and train it under irregular conditions.

6. Potential impacts
This project has the potential to reduce total delays at intersections, reducing the productivity loss. However, there is also a potential that reduced traffic simply induces more car demand, making the city worse off from encouraged car usage and lowered interest in public transit investments.
